V5 Linear SVM — what the results mean (VAL + TEST)
	•	SVM beats Logistic Regression, but it’s still far behind the transformer.
	•	VAL: accuracy 0.558 (vs LogReg 0.495), macro-F1 0.291 (vs 0.267)
	•	TEST: accuracy 0.556 (vs LogReg 0.509), macro-F1 0.291 (vs 0.275)
So SVM is a stronger classical baseline, but it still can’t capture deep context like DistilRoBERTa (v2) or the simplified-label v3.
	•	It’s especially good at “mentioned_only” and “annoyance”.
	•	mentioned_only F1: 0.738 (VAL), 0.712 (TEST) with high recall (~0.80).
	•	annoyance F1: 0.665 (VAL), 0.708 (TEST) with high recall (~0.72–0.79).
These are emotions that often correlate with obvious lexical patterns (complaint words, negation, intensifiers, “just mentioned”).
	•	Moderate improvements on disappointment vs LogReg; mixed on admiration.
	•	disappointment F1 improves a lot on TEST (0.386 vs LogReg 0.364) and on VAL (0.315 vs 0.292).
	•	admiration is inconsistent: VAL F1 0.364 (better than LogReg 0.311), but TEST F1 0.337 (roughly similar to LogReg 0.312).
	•	Neutral and satisfaction are still weak / confusing.
	•	Neutral: VAL F1 0.314, TEST 0.193 (drops on test).
	•	Satisfaction: VAL F1 0.221, TEST 0.286 (improves on test but still low).
This is the classical “bag-of-words” limitation: neutral and mild satisfaction often share vocabulary with other classes, and subtle cues depend on context/structure.
	•	Rare labels remain “dead” (expected).
Disgust / gratitude / mixed_emotions have 0–3 examples → SVM can’t learn them, so F1 stays 0 and drags macro-F1 down.

⸻

Why SVM > LogReg here (intuition)
	•	Linear SVM optimizes a margin (separation boundary) which often works very well with high-dimensional sparse TF-IDF features.
	•	Logistic Regression outputs calibrated probabilities, but in sparse text settings SVM frequently has an edge for pure classification accuracy.
	•	Both still share the same core limitation: no deep contextual semantics, only surface word/phrase patterns.
